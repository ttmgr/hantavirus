    About Hantaviruses

    Overview

    Hantaviruses are a family of RNA viruses transmitted primarily through inhalation of aerosolized rodent excreta (urine, droppings, saliva). They cause two main clinical syndromes: hantavirus pulmonary syndrome (HPS) in the Americas and hemorrhagic fever with renal syndrome (HFRS) in Europe and Asia.

    Major Serotypes

    Andes virus (ANDV) -- South America, primarily Argentina and Chile. Causes HPS. The only hantavirus with documented human-to-human transmission. Case fatality rate approximately 30-40%.

    Sin Nombre virus (SNV) -- North America, predominantly southwestern United States. Causes HPS. No documented person-to-person spread. Case fatality rate approximately 36%.

    Puumala virus (PUUV) -- Europe, especially Scandinavia, Germany, and the Balkans. Causes a milder form of HFRS (nephropathia epidemica). Carried by bank voles. Case fatality rate below 1%.

    Hantaan virus (HTNV) -- East Asia, primarily China and Korea. Causes classical HFRS. Case fatality rate 5-15%.

    Transmission

    Primary route: inhalation of aerosolized virus from rodent urine, droppings, or nesting materials. Risk factors include cleaning enclosed spaces with rodent infestation, agricultural work, and camping. Andes virus is exceptional in that it can spread between humans through close contact, though this remains rare.

    Symptoms

    HPS: fever, myalgia, and gastrointestinal symptoms progressing to cough and rapid respiratory failure. HFRS: fever, hemorrhagic manifestations, and acute kidney injury. Incubation period typically 1-5 weeks.

    Prevention

    No approved vaccine is available for human use. Prevention relies on rodent control, sealing entry points in buildings, safe cleanup of rodent-contaminated areas (wet methods, ventilation, PPE), and avoiding contact with wild rodents. In endemic areas, public health campaigns focus on reducing rodent habitat around human dwellings.
