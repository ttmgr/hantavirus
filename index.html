    About Hantaviruses

    Hantaviruses are a family of RNA viruses transmitted primarily through inhalation of aerosolized rodent excreta (urine, droppings, saliva). They cause two main clinical syndromes: hantavirus pulmonary syndrome (HPS) in the Americas and hemorrhagic fever with renal syndrome (HFRS) in Europe and Asia.

    Andes virus (ANDV) -- South America. The only hantavirus with documented human-to-human transmission. CFR 30-40%.

    Sin Nombre virus (SNV) -- North America, southwestern US. CFR ~36%. No person-to-person spread.

    Puumala virus (PUUV) -- Europe. Milder HFRS (nephropathia epidemica). Carried by bank voles. CFR below 1%.

    Hantaan virus (HTNV) -- East Asia. Classical HFRS. CFR 5-15%.

    Primary route: inhalation of aerosolized virus from rodent excreta. Risk factors include cleaning enclosed spaces with rodent infestation, agricultural work, and camping. Andes virus can exceptionally spread between humans through close contact.

    HPS: fever, myalgia, gastrointestinal symptoms progressing to respiratory failure. HFRS: fever, hemorrhage, acute kidney injury. Incubation 1-5 weeks. Early supportive care improves survival significantly.

    No approved vaccine available. Prevention: rodent control, sealing building entry points, safe cleanup of contaminated areas (wet methods, ventilation, PPE), avoiding wild rodent contact.
